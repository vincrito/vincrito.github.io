Pancreatic Cancer

A comprehensive guide to understanding pancreatic cancer — from diagnosis and staging through treatment, surgery, recovery, and support.

Anatomy

Understanding the Pancreas

The pancreas is an organ located in the upper abdomen behind the stomach and colon, next to the spleen. It has two main jobs: regulating blood sugar through hormones like insulin, and producing digestive enzymes that break down fat, protein, and sugars. Anatomically it is divided into the head, neck, body, tail, and uncinate process, and is in close proximity to several major blood vessels supplying the liver, spleen, and intestines.

Pancreatic Exocrine Insufficiency

When the pancreas cannot produce enough digestive enzymes — or enzymes are blocked from reaching the small intestine — the body cannot adequately digest food. This is called pancreatic exocrine insufficiency. Common symptoms include frequent or loose stools, pale or oily stools, bloating, cramping, and unintentional weight loss. Your doctor may prescribe pancreatic enzyme replacement therapy (PERT) capsules taken with meals.

Pancreatic Cancer and Blood Sugar

The pancreas produces insulin to lower blood sugar after meals. When cancer or surgery impairs insulin production, blood sugar can rise and diabetes may develop. Regular blood sugar monitoring is an important part of care both before and after pancreatic surgery.

Overview

What Is Pancreatic Cancer?

Pancreatic cancer is an abnormal, disorganized growth of cells that lose their normal function and replicate rapidly. It can invade neighboring organs and spread to other areas of the body. The most common type is adenocarcinoma; adenosquamous carcinoma and neuroendocrine tumors (PNET) are less common variants.

Potential Symptoms

Painless jaundice (yellowing of skin/eyes)
Pain in the abdomen and/or back
Significant unexplained weight loss
New-onset or worsening diabetes
Low appetite, indigestion, nausea
Clay-colored, oily, or foul-smelling stools

Risk Factors

Who Is at Risk?

Most cases of pancreatic cancer have no single identifiable cause. Known risk factors include:

  • Chronic or hereditary pancreatitis
  • Family history of pancreatic cancer
  • Smoking
  • Long-standing diabetes
  • Inherited gene mutations (BRCA1/2, PALB2, ATM, Lynch syndrome)
  • Obesity and sedentary lifestyle

Pancreatic cancer is treated by a team of specialists — surgeons, medical oncologists, radiation oncologists, gastroenterologists, radiologists, pathologists, geneticists, social workers, and nutritionists. Consulting a high-volume specialist center leads to better outcomes.

Diagnosis

How Pancreatic Cancer Is Diagnosed

Once a diagnosis of pancreatic cancer is suspected, several tests are used to confirm the diagnosis and determine how advanced the cancer is. Precise staging information is essential to designing the most appropriate treatment plan.

Imaging Studies

CT Scan

The primary tool for detecting a pancreatic tumor, assessing its size and location, and evaluating whether it has spread. CT scans also determine whether a tumor can be safely removed surgically, providing the roadmap for operative planning. A contrast dye is typically given through an IV. The scan takes about 15 minutes.

MRI & MRCP

MRI uses magnets and radio waves — no radiation — to create detailed images. It is particularly useful for clarifying liver lesions and evaluating the bile and pancreatic ducts. MRCP (Magnetic Resonance Cholangiopancreatography) is a specific MRI that focuses on detailed images of the pancreas and bile duct anatomy. The scan can take up to one hour and requires removal of all metal objects and devices.

PET Scan

A PET scan examines how the body uses energy. A small amount of radioactive sugar (FDG) is injected through an IV; cancer cells, which use more energy, appear as bright spots. PET scans provide additional information when CT or MRI results are unclear. Preparation includes fasting for 4 hours before the scan. The entire process takes 2–3 hours.

Tumor Markers

CA 19-9

The primary blood test used to monitor pancreatic cancer. Levels often rise when cancer is growing and decrease when treatment is working. It is not a perfect test — some people never produce CA 19-9, and benign conditions can also cause it to rise. Doctors primarily use it to track changes over time.

CEA

Carcinoembryonic antigen is less reliable than CA 19-9 but can provide additional information, particularly in patients who do not produce CA 19-9. When followed alongside CA 19-9, it can help characterize how the cancer is behaving and responding to treatment.

Biopsy & Procedures

EUS-FNA (Endoscopic Ultrasound – Fine Needle Aspiration)

An EUS uses sound waves to create detailed pictures of the digestive system, including the pancreas. A thin, flexible tube is passed through the mouth into the first part of the small intestine, allowing doctors to visualize the pancreas up close. EUS can determine the size and location of a tumor and whether it has spread to nearby lymph nodes. It can also take a tissue sample (biopsy) using a fine needle — a process called FNA or core needle biopsy. The tissue is sent to a pathologist who examines it under a microscope to detect cancer cells and identify their type. EUS is typically an outpatient procedure; the patient goes home the same day.

ERCP / Stent

ERCP (Endoscopic Retrograde Cholangiopancreatography) allows doctors to visualize and treat problems in the bile and pancreatic ducts. In pancreatic cancer, one of the most common early problems is a blockage of the bile duct, which causes jaundice, itching, and sometimes infection. During ERCP, a small tube called a stent is placed to open the blocked duct and allow bile to flow normally again. ERCP is often performed together with EUS in a single session — the EUS locates the tumor and guides the biopsy, while ERCP relieves the obstruction.

Important

Getting a Tissue Diagnosis

A tissue biopsy is usually required before starting chemotherapy or other non-surgical treatment. For patients who will go directly to surgery, a biopsy is generally not needed beforehand. Your multidisciplinary team will determine the right sequence for your situation. If a biopsy is inconclusive, it may need to be repeated.

Staging

How Is Pancreatic Cancer Staged?

Staging determines how far a cancer has progressed and whether it has spread. It is the foundation for all treatment decisions.

Imaging Staging

CT, MRI, and PET scans are used to determine the size and location of the tumor, whether it has grown into nearby blood vessels or organs (local invasion), and whether it has spread to distant organs (metastatic disease).

Staging Laparoscopy

A short minimally invasive surgery through small abdominal incisions. The surgeon looks directly at the liver surface and abdominal cavity for small areas of cancer spread that may not appear on scans. If hidden spread is found, major surgery is avoided and treatment focuses on systemic therapy instead.

TNM System (AJCC)

T (Tumor): How large and how far it has grown. N (Nodes): Whether it has spread to lymph nodes. M (Metastasis): Whether it has spread to distant organs. These are combined into an overall stage (I–IV).

Resectability Staging

Resectable

No involvement of major arteries or veins. Surgery may be performed upfront or after a short course of neoadjuvant chemotherapy, depending on the multidisciplinary team's assessment and tumor biology.

Borderline Resectable

The tumor abuts but does not encase major blood vessels. Neoadjuvant chemotherapy (typically FOLFIRINOX or gemcitabine/nab-paclitaxel) is given first to improve the chance of a complete resection.

Locally Advanced / Metastatic

Arterial encasement or distant metastases preclude upfront surgery. Systemic chemotherapy is the primary treatment. A subset of locally advanced patients who respond well may later become surgical candidates.

Clinical staging is based on imaging and pre-surgical exams. Pathological staging — based on what is seen under the microscope after surgery — can differ and provides more precise information. Accurate staging is essential because it determines the most effective sequence of surgery, chemotherapy, and radiation.

Genetics

Genetic Considerations

About 1 in 10 cases of pancreatic cancer are inherited — passed down through families due to changes in DNA. Knowing your family history can help you understand your risks and take steps to protect your health.

What Is Genetic Testing?

Genetic testing looks at your DNA to find inherited changes that might raise your risk of pancreatic and other cancers. A simple blood or saliva test can help doctors find these changes and inform a plan for early detection and prevention. Genetic testing can also determine whether specific targeted therapies would be beneficial in patients with metastatic pancreatic cancer.

Who Should Be Tested?

It is strongly recommended that all patients with pancreatic cancer undergo genetic testing — both germline (inherited) and somatic (tumor) testing. A genetic counselor will guide you through the process and help you understand the results.

If You Have Elevated Risk

Steps You Can Take

  • Regular check-ups and surveillance imaging (MRI or endoscopic ultrasound)
  • Eat well, stay active, and avoid smoking
  • Consider participation in clinical trials studying early detection
  • Talk to a genetic counselor about your family history

Treatment

Chemotherapy

Chemotherapy uses medicines that travel through the bloodstream to prevent cancer cells from growing and spreading. It can be given before surgery (neoadjuvant), after surgery (adjuvant), or as the primary treatment when surgery is not an option.

Chemotherapy drugs can be given intravenously (through a vein) or by mouth, usually as outpatient treatment. The most commonly used agents in pancreatic cancer are fluorouracil (5-FU) and gemcitabine. The two most common combination regimens are FOLFIRINOX and Gem-Abraxane (gemcitabine/nab-paclitaxel).

Chemotherapy may be given alone or in combination with surgery, targeted therapy, immunotherapy, and/or radiation. Genetic testing results can influence which chemotherapy regimen is most appropriate.

Mediport (Port-a-Cath)

Because many chemotherapy treatments are given through an IV, doctors often place a small device called a mediport under the skin of the chest. It connects to a thin tube leading to a large vein, allowing nurses to give chemotherapy and draw blood without repeated needle sticks. The port is placed during a short outpatient procedure with light sedation, and can stay in place for as long as chemotherapy is needed. It must be flushed regularly to keep it working properly.

Side Effects

Side effects depend on the drugs used, dosage, and duration. Your care team will help you manage them.

Fatigue & Anemia — Low red blood cell count causes tiredness and shortness of breath. Rest, nutrition, and treatment for anemia help.
Nausea & Vomiting — Anti-nausea medications, small bland meals, and clear fluids help. Report persistent symptoms to your doctor.
Hair Loss — Usually begins a few weeks after treatment starts. Hair almost always grows back after chemotherapy ends.
Diarrhea / Stomach Cramps — Stay hydrated, eat small mild meals, avoid spicy foods. Notify your doctor if it persists.
Low Blood Counts — Fewer white cells (infection risk), red cells (fatigue), or platelets (bleeding risk). Blood counts are checked regularly throughout treatment.
Neuropathy — Tingling, numbness, or pain in hands/feet from nerve effects. Tell your doctor promptly — dose adjustments can prevent worsening.
Changes in Taste / Mouth Sores — Gentle brushing, saltwater rinses, and soft foods help. Avoid spicy or acidic foods.
Hand-Foot Syndrome — Redness, swelling, or peeling of hands/feet. Cool water, gentle moisturizers, and comfortable shoes help.

Treatment

Immunotherapy

Immunotherapy helps the body's own immune system recognize and attack cancer cells. While immunotherapy has shown success in several other cancers, its benefit in pancreatic cancer has so far been limited — most pancreatic tumors do not respond well to immunotherapy alone.

An important exception is patients whose tumors have a specific genetic feature called MSI-H (microsatellite instability-high) or dMMR (mismatch repair deficiency) — these tumors can respond well to immunotherapy. This is one reason genetic testing of the tumor is recommended for all patients. Clinical trials are actively testing new approaches combining immunotherapy with chemotherapy, radiation, or targeted drugs.

Treatment

Radiation Therapy

Radiation therapy uses high-energy X-rays to kill cancer cells and stop them from growing, aimed at a specific area of the body. Your doctor will determine whether radiation is appropriate as part of your treatment plan.

Why Radiation Is Used

Radiation may be used before surgery to shrink a tumor, after surgery to destroy remaining cancer cells, to treat locally advanced tumors near critical blood vessels, or to relieve pain and other symptoms when surgery is not possible.

Techniques

IMRT (Intensity Modulated Radiation Therapy)Shapes radiation beams to match the exact size and form of the tumor, delivering high doses directly to cancer while reducing impact on surrounding healthy tissue.
SBRT (Stereotactic Body Radiation Therapy)Delivers a higher concentrated dose over about 5 sessions. Precisely targets the pancreatic tumor while protecting the sensitive bowel and stomach nearby.
VMAT / IGRTAdvanced delivery techniques that use continuous motion or imaging guidance to maximize precision and minimize treatment time and radiation to healthy tissue.

What to Expect

ConsultationYour radiation oncologist reviews your case, answers questions, and performs a physical exam.
SimulationA special CT scanner maps the tumor and nearby organs so you are positioned precisely the same way for every treatment.
PlanningThe care team outlines the exact tumor area and designs your personalized treatment plan. This takes about 1–2 weeks.
TreatmentEach session takes about 30 minutes (10 minutes of actual radiation). You lie still; you will not see, feel, or hear the radiation.

Common Side Effects

Fatigue, nausea, decreased appetite, loose stools, skin irritation in the treated area, and weight loss. Rare effects include intestinal damage or bile duct strictures. Your care team monitors for both short-term and long-term side effects.

Treatment

Surgery

Surgery combined with systemic treatment offers the best chance of cure for patients with pancreatic cancer. The goal is complete removal of the tumor with negative margins.

Who Is Eligible?

Surgery is considered for patients who are generally fit for major surgery and do not have metastatic (distant) disease. The first step is assessing resectability — whether the tumor can be completely removed without injury to critical structures. Patients with metastatic disease do not benefit from surgery and are treated with systemic therapy.

Types of Pancreatic Surgery

Pancreaticoduodenectomy (Whipple Procedure)

For tumors in the head of the pancreas. The head of the pancreas, duodenum, gallbladder, a portion of the bile duct, and part of the stomach are removed. The remaining organs are reconnected to restore digestive function. Operative time is typically 4–6 hours.

Distal Pancreatectomy

For tumors in the body or tail. The body and tail of the pancreas, along with the spleen, are removed. Minimally invasive (laparoscopic or robotic) approaches are used when feasible, with generally shorter recovery than the Whipple.

Total Pancreatectomy

Performed when the tumor affects the entire pancreas or critical vessels cannot be preserved. The entire pancreas is removed. Patients will need lifelong insulin therapy and careful blood sugar management.

Surgical Approaches

Open SurgeryThe traditional approach. A single incision in the abdomen gives the surgeon direct access to the pancreas and surrounding structures.
Laparoscopic SurgerySmall incisions with a camera and specialized tools. The surgeon sees the pancreas on a screen and removes the tumor without a large incision.
Robotic SurgeryA specialized laparoscopic approach using a robotic system that provides a 3D view and allows the surgeon to operate with greater precision and stability.

Resectability should be determined by a multidisciplinary team at a high-volume center using protocol-quality imaging. Assessments vary between institutions — a second opinion is appropriate and often valuable.

After Surgery

Recovery

In the Hospital

Right after surgery, patients are monitored closely in a hospital room (sometimes briefly in the ICU). Patients are encouraged to get up and start moving the day after surgery, gradually increasing activity each day. Most people can go home once bowel function returns — typically 7–10 days after surgery for a Whipple.

At Home

Full recovery takes about 2 months. Recovery speed depends on overall health before surgery, the type of surgery, and whether any complications arise. During follow-up visits, the care team monitors the incision, diet, bowel function, and pain. Imaging may be done to check healing and ensure the cancer has not returned.

Adjuvant Chemotherapy

Chemotherapy after surgery (adjuvant chemotherapy) is typically recommended to reduce the risk of recurrence. It usually begins 4–8 weeks after surgery, once recovery is complete.

Potential Complications

Delayed gastric emptying — Very common; the stomach takes longer to empty. Usually improves with time and medication.
Anastomotic leak — A leak from where organs were reconnected. Treated with drains, antibiotics, or occasionally a procedure.
Infections — Treated with antibiotics or drainage of infected areas.
Blood clots — Blood-thinning medication and early walking reduce this risk.
Diabetes (long-term) — Removing part or all of the pancreas can cause diabetes. Managed with insulin and dietary changes.
Exocrine insufficiency (long-term) — The pancreas may not make enough digestive enzymes. Treated with pancreatic enzyme pills (PERT) taken with meals.

Research

Clinical Trials

Clinical trials are research studies that investigate new treatments or new combinations of treatments. In the fight against pancreatic cancer, clinical trials provide early access to innovative therapies designed to improve effectiveness and minimize side effects — benefiting current participants and future patients while advancing research.

Clinical trial eligibility varies widely by stage and tumor type. If you are interested in exploring available trials, ask your doctor which trials you may be eligible for. Your care team at Mount Sinai has access to the full spectrum of open trials.

Supportive Care

Nutrition

Good nutrition is important during cancer treatment. Eating the right foods helps you stay strong, tolerate treatment better, and maintain a healthy weight.

Why Nutrition Matters

For patients with pancreatic cancer, good nutrition helps prevent weight loss and malnutrition, maintain treatment doses and schedule, and manage side effects from both cancer and treatment.

Managing Weight Loss

Poor appetiteEat small, frequent meals and snacks every 2–3 hours. Choose high-calorie, high-protein foods. Consider oral nutrition supplements.
MalabsorptionYour doctor may prescribe pancreatic enzyme pills (PERT) to take with meals. Smaller, more frequent meals also aid digestion.
Tumor-related weight lossFocus on nutrient-dense foods, stay active to preserve muscle, and ask your care team about appetite-stimulating medications.

Eating Tips by Symptom

Nausea & VomitingSmall meals every few hours; bland foods (toast, crackers, rice, potatoes); cold or room-temperature foods; ginger; avoid fatty, spicy, or high-fiber foods.
DiarrheaDrink plenty of fluids; eat low-fiber foods (bananas, white rice, applesauce, toast, yogurt); avoid caffeine, alcohol, raw fruits and vegetables, and excessive dairy.
ConstipationDrink at least 8 cups of fluids daily; eat high-fiber foods (whole grains, fruits, vegetables, beans); exercise regularly.
Sore MouthSoft, moist, or blended foods; add liquids to foods; rinse with salt water or Biotene; avoid spicy, tart, very hot, or very cold foods.
Poor Appetite with Weight LossAdd calories with olive oil, cheese, eggs, avocado, and nut butters. Add protein with yogurt, nuts, beans, and protein powder. Eat small meals every 2–3 hours.

A registered dietitian on the pancreatic cancer team can create a personalized nutrition plan. Ask your care team for a referral.

Supportive Care

Social Work & Emotional Support

What Medical Social Workers Do

Medical social workers assess the emotional and social needs of patients and families, collaborate with the healthcare team on treatment plans, and provide counseling and resources to help cope with the effects of illness. They also help patients access community services such as financial assistance, transportation, lodging, and emotional support.

Coping Strategies

Healthy coping strategies lead to longer-lasting positive outcomes. What works varies from person to person — it's important to build a toolkit that works for you.

  • Expressing emotions — Allowing yourself to feel and name your emotions increases resilience. "I feel…" statements are a powerful tool.
  • Connecting with others — Loved ones, support groups, and counselors reduce stress and increase wellbeing.
  • Setting boundaries — Communicating your needs and saying "no" preserves energy for what matters most.
  • Relaxation techniques — Mindfulness and deep breathing slow the heart rate and reduce stress (e.g. Calm app).
  • Faith & community — Spiritual practice and faith communities can provide meaning and comfort during difficult life events.

Advance Care Planning

An advance directive is a legal document that explains what kind of medical care you want if you are ever too sick or unable to speak for yourself. It helps your doctors and family know what to do.

Health Care Proxy — Appoints someone you trust to make medical decisions for you if you cannot speak for yourself.
Living Will — Documents what medical treatments you do or do not want if you become seriously ill.
Five Wishes — A type of advance directive covering medical, personal, emotional, and spiritual wishes.
DNR / DNI Order — Hospital-based orders directing doctors not to perform CPR or use a breathing machine if needed.

Ask your social worker or care team to help you complete advance directives. It is an act of care for yourself and your loved ones.

Supportive Care

Palliative Care & Spiritual Support

What Is Palliative Care?

Palliative care focuses on comfort, quality of life, and your total well-being during and after cancer treatment. It is not only for end-of-life — it can and should begin at the time of diagnosis and accompany any active cancer treatment. It helps relieve symptoms and side effects and supports you and your family throughout the journey.

Always ask your healthcare team about palliative care services — they are available and they can help significantly with quality of life.

Spiritual Care

A cancer diagnosis can be overwhelming not only physically but emotionally and spiritually. Spiritual care supports your inner life, beliefs, and values regardless of your faith background. It may include prayer or meditation, talking with a chaplain or spiritual advisor, exploring questions of meaning and hope, or finding peace in music, nature, or personal rituals.

Ask your nurse or doctor to connect you with the hospital chaplain service at Mount Sinai.
Reach out to a pastor, rabbi, imam, priest, or spiritual leader you trust — many offer counseling or prayer visits.
CancerCare and PanCAN both offer counseling and spiritual support resources.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if my pancreatic cancer is resectable?
Resectability is determined by high-quality multiphase CT imaging. The key question is whether the tumor involves major blood vessels — the superior mesenteric artery, celiac axis, portal vein, or superior mesenteric vein. Tumors clearly away from these vessels are resectable. Those abutting but not encasing vessels are borderline resectable. This distinction is best made at a high-volume center with a multidisciplinary team. A second opinion is appropriate if you have any doubt about the assessment you have received.
What is the Whipple procedure?
The Whipple procedure (pancreaticoduodenectomy) removes the head of the pancreas, duodenum, gallbladder, a portion of the bile duct, and sometimes part of the stomach. The remaining structures are reconnected to restore digestive continuity. It is the standard operation for cancers in the head of the pancreas, takes 4–6 hours, and is best performed at high-volume centers where outcomes are significantly better.
Should I have chemotherapy before surgery?
For borderline resectable disease, neoadjuvant (preoperative) chemotherapy is strongly recommended to improve the chance of a complete resection. For clearly resectable tumors, both upfront surgery and a neoadjuvant approach are valid — the choice depends on performance status, tumor biology, CA 19-9, and multidisciplinary team input. Many high-volume centers now favor neoadjuvant chemotherapy even for resectable tumors to select patients most likely to benefit from surgery.
What if the cancer is not resectable?
Locally advanced disease is treated with systemic chemotherapy, sometimes followed by radiation. A subset of patients who respond well may become candidates for surgery after 4–6 months — reassessed with repeat imaging. Metastatic disease is managed with chemotherapy; surgery is not typically performed. Palliative interventions may address biliary obstruction or gastric outlet obstruction when needed.
Is pancreatic cancer hereditary?
About 1 in 10 cases are hereditary. All patients with pancreatic cancer should undergo germline and somatic genetic testing. Inherited mutations in BRCA1, BRCA2, PALB2, ATM, and Lynch syndrome genes can not only inform family members' risk but also guide treatment decisions — some mutations make pancreatic cancers sensitive to specific chemotherapy agents or targeted therapies.
What is the survival rate after pancreatic cancer surgery?
Five-year survival after surgical resection with adjuvant chemotherapy is approximately 20–25%, depending on margin status, lymph node involvement, and treatment response. While this may seem low, surgery remains the only potentially curative treatment, and outcomes have meaningfully improved with modern chemotherapy regimens (FOLFIRINOX, gemcitabine/nab-paclitaxel). At high-volume centers, outcomes are consistently better than at low-volume institutions.

Get an Expert Evaluation

Resectability assessments vary between centers. If you or a family member has been diagnosed with pancreatic cancer — or told it is not operable — a second opinion is appropriate.
