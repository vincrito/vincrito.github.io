Pancreatic Cancer

Surgical evaluation, resectability assessment, and coordinated multidisciplinary treatment for pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma.

Overview

What Is Pancreatic Cancer?

Pancreatic cancer most commonly refers to pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC), which arises from the ductal cells of the exocrine pancreas. It accounts for approximately 90% of pancreatic malignancies and is among the most challenging cancers to treat because it is frequently diagnosed at an advanced stage.

The pancreas is a gland that sits behind the stomach. Tumors most commonly arise in the head of the pancreas (60–70%), where they often obstruct the bile duct and cause jaundice. Tumors of the body and tail (30–40%) tend to grow silently and are frequently more advanced at diagnosis.

Only about 15–20% of patients have surgically resectable disease at the time of diagnosis. The surgical evaluation — determining whether the tumor can be safely and completely removed — is one of the most critical decisions in the management of this disease.

Signs and Symptoms

Symptoms vary by tumor location and are often nonspecific early in the course of the disease, which contributes to late diagnosis.

Painless jaundice (yellowing of skin/eyes)
Mid-back or abdominal pain
Unintentional weight loss
New-onset or worsening diabetes
Loss of appetite, nausea
Light-colored stools, dark urine

Workup

How It Is Diagnosed

The diagnostic evaluation begins with cross-sectional imaging and is completed with multidisciplinary review before any treatment decision is made.

CT scan (multiphase) — The primary tool for staging and resectability assessment. Evaluates vascular involvement and distant disease.
MRI/MRCP — Useful for biliary anatomy, liver lesions, and cases where CT is equivocal.
EUS (endoscopic ultrasound) — Allows fine-needle biopsy for tissue diagnosis when needed, particularly before neoadjuvant therapy.
CA 19-9 — A serum tumor marker useful for following response to treatment, though not diagnostic on its own.

Surgical Decision-Making

When Is Surgery Appropriate?

The critical first step is staging — classifying the tumor as resectable, borderline resectable, locally advanced, or metastatic. Each category has a different treatment pathway.

Resectable

No involvement of major arteries or veins. Surgery may be performed upfront or after a short course of neoadjuvant chemotherapy depending on multidisciplinary team discussion and tumor biology.

Borderline Resectable

The tumor abuts major blood vessels but does not encase them. Neoadjuvant chemotherapy (typically FOLFIRINOX or gemcitabine/nab-paclitaxel) is given first to improve the margin of resection and select patients most likely to benefit from surgery.

Locally Advanced / Metastatic

Arterial encasement or distant metastases preclude upfront surgery. Systemic chemotherapy is the primary treatment. A subset of locally advanced patients may become candidates for surgery after a favorable response.

The determination of resectability should be made by a multidisciplinary team at a high-volume center using protocol-quality imaging. Resectability assessments vary between institutions, and a second opinion is appropriate and often valuable.

The Operations

What to Expect

The two main operations for pancreatic cancer depend on where the tumor is located.

Pancreaticoduodenectomy (Whipple Procedure)

For tumors in the head of the pancreas. The operation removes the head of the pancreas, the duodenum, the gallbladder, a portion of the bile duct, and often the first portion of the stomach. The remaining pancreas, bile duct, and stomach are reconnected to the small intestine. Operative time is typically 5–7 hours. Hospital stay is 5–7 days in straightforward cases.

Distal Pancreatectomy

For tumors of the body or tail. The left side of the pancreas is removed, usually along with the spleen. Minimally invasive (laparoscopic or robotic) approaches are used when feasible. Recovery is generally shorter than the Whipple.

Recovery

What Recovery Looks Like

Most patients are out of bed by day 2 and eating a soft diet by day 3–4. Hospital stay is typically 5–7 days for an uncomplicated Whipple.

Full recovery to normal activity takes 4–6 weeks. Adjuvant chemotherapy typically begins 4–8 weeks after surgery once recovery is complete.

Pancreatic enzyme replacement therapy (PERT) is almost always needed after a Whipple. Some patients develop new or worsened diabetes.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if my pancreatic cancer is resectable?
Resectability is determined by high-quality cross-sectional CT imaging. The key question is whether the tumor involves major blood vessels — the superior mesenteric artery, celiac axis, portal vein, or superior mesenteric vein. Tumors clearly away from these vessels are resectable. Those abutting but not encasing vessels are borderline resectable. This distinction is best made at a high-volume center with a multidisciplinary team.
What is the Whipple procedure?
The Whipple procedure (pancreaticoduodenectomy) removes the head of the pancreas, duodenum, gallbladder, a portion of the bile duct, and sometimes part of the stomach. The remaining structures are reconnected to restore digestive continuity. It is the standard operation for cancers in the head of the pancreas and is best performed at high-volume centers.
Should I have chemotherapy before surgery?
For borderline resectable disease, neoadjuvant chemotherapy before surgery is strongly recommended. For clearly resectable tumors, both upfront surgery and neoadjuvant approaches are valid — the choice depends on performance status, tumor biology, and multidisciplinary team review. Many high-volume centers now favor neoadjuvant chemotherapy even for resectable tumors.
What if the cancer is not resectable?
Locally advanced disease is treated with systemic chemotherapy and sometimes radiation. A subset of patients who respond well may become candidates for surgery after 4–6 months — this is reassessed with repeat imaging. Metastatic disease is managed with chemotherapy; surgery is not typically performed.
What is the survival rate after pancreatic cancer surgery?
Five-year survival after surgical resection with adjuvant chemotherapy is approximately 20–25%, depending on margin status, lymph node involvement, and treatment response. While this may seem low, surgery remains the only potentially curative treatment, and outcomes have meaningfully improved with modern chemotherapy regimens.
